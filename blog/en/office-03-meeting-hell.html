That Meeting Should Have Been an Email: How Corporate America Wastes 31 Hours a Month

June 26, 2026 · 5 min read

31 Hours. Almost an Entire Work Week.

According to a 2025 Atlassian study, the average knowledge worker attends 62 meetings per month — and considers 50% of them a waste of time. That's 31 hours. Almost four full workdays. Gone. The meeting-industrial complex is eating American productivity alive, and we're all just nodding along on mute.

Meetings feel productive without being productive. The organizer feels important. Attendees feel included. Nobody has to do actual work for 30 minutes. It's the perfect crime. Managers especially fall into the trap: scheduling meetings becomes a substitute for managing.

The 7 Types of Completely Unnecessary Meetings

  • The Status Update Parade — everyone reads aloud what they could have typed in Slack. • The 'Let's Align' Marathon — 45 minutes to agree on something two people could have decided in chat. • The Meeting Before the Meeting — to prepare for the real meeting. • The Post-Mortem That Could Have Been a Google Form. • The Weekly Sync No One Prepares For. • The Brainstorm Where Everyone Multitasks on Email. • The 'Quick Catch-Up' That Takes An Hour.

If you recognize 5 out of 7, you're in meeting culture hell.

How to Escape the Meeting Trap

  • Decline with purpose: 'I'll review the recording and async any questions.' • Request an agenda: 'Could you share what decisions we need to make?' • Block 'Deep Work' time on your calendar as recurring, non-negotiable appointments. • The nuclear option: 'Can this be an email?' Used correctly, it's the most powerful four words in corporate English.

In Southeast Asian offices, a packed calendar signals status — the more meetings you're in, the more important you appear. Breaking this culture requires scheduling your own deep work blocks first and treating them as seriously as a board meeting.
