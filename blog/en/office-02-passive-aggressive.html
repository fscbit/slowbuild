'Per My Last Email' — The Complete Dictionary of Corporate Passive Aggression

June 26, 2026 · 6 min read

The Most Dangerous Phrase in Corporate America

'Per my last email.' Three words that have launched a thousand HR complaints. On the surface, it's a simple reference to previous correspondence. In reality, it's corporate shorthand for 'I already told you this, you just didn't read it, and now I have paper receipts.' Passive-aggressive communication has become the primary weapon in modern office warfare — and everyone's armed.

In hierarchical workplaces where direct confrontation is career suicide, coded language becomes survival dialect. You can't tell your boss 'that's a terrible idea' — but you can say 'interesting approach, has anyone explored the risks?' The office has perfected the art of saying 'go to hell' in a way that makes the recipient look forward to the trip.

Tier 1: The Gentle Reminders (Mildly Annoying)

  • 'Just circling back on this' = I've been waiting and you're ignoring me. • 'Wanted to bump this to the top of your inbox' = Your inbox isn't the problem, you are. • 'Friendly reminder' = This is NOT friendly. • 'As per our conversation' = I'm documenting this for when you pretend we never spoke.

These are the gateway drug of office passive aggression — annoying but rarely lethal.

Tier 2: Escalation Phrases (Starting to Sting)

  • 'Going forward, let's...' = You messed up and I'm creating a new rule just for you. • 'I might be misunderstanding, but...' = I understand perfectly and you're wrong. • 'With all due respect' = Zero respect follows. • 'I'll take that under advisement' = I will immediately forget you said this.

Tier 3: Nuclear Options (HR Territory)

  • 'Let me know if I can clarify anything' = You're not smart enough to understand. • 'I'm surprised by your interpretation' = You're either lying or incompetent. • 'Perhaps we should involve [boss's name]' = I'm threatening to tell on you.

Use Tier 3 phrases sparingly — they leave permanent scars on professional relationships.

The Southeast Asian Edition: Passive Aggression With a Smile

In Singapore and Malaysia, passive aggression comes gift-wrapped. 'Can lah, but...' = Absolutely not. 'I'll try my best' = I won't. 'See how first' = Won't happen. The 'lah'/'lor' particles add an entire second layer of meaning that expats spend years decoding. Combined with the cultural premium on harmony and 'face,' SEA office workers have elevated passive aggression to a performance art.
